## Unit 1: Introduction to Economics

### 1.1 What is Economics?

- **Core concepts** — The 9 key concepts used throughout the course (and as IA lenses): _Scarcity, Choice, Efficiency, Equity, Economic well-being, Sustainability, Change, Interdependence, Intervention._

- **The problem of choice:**
  - _Factors of production_ — Land, Labour, Capital, Entrepreneurship.
  - _Scarcity_ — Unlimited wants, limited resources. The fundamental economic problem.
  - _Opportunity cost_ — Value of the next best alternative foregone. Every choice has one.
  - _Free goods_ — Zero opportunity cost (e.g. air). Nearly all goods are economic goods.
  - _Basic economic questions_ — What to produce? How to produce? For whom to produce?
  - _Economic systems_ — Free market (price mechanism), planned (government), mixed (both).

#### The Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

- **Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)** — Shows maximum output combinations of two goods when all resources are fully and efficiently employed.

- **Interpreting the PPC:**
  - _On the curve_ — Productively efficient.
  - _Inside the curve_ — Unemployed or inefficiently used resources.
  - _Outside the curve_ — Currently unattainable with existing resources and technology.
  - _Opportunity cost_ — Moving along the curve shows what must be sacrificed to gain more of the other good.
  - _Concave PPC (standard)_ — Increasing opportunity cost because resources are not perfectly substitutable.
  - _Straight-line PPC_ — Constant opportunity cost because resources are assumed perfectly substitutable; this is unrealistic but useful for comparison.

- **Economic growth and the PPC:**
  - _Actual growth_ — A movement from a point inside the curve to a point on the boundary as idle resources are employed.
  - _Growth in production possibilities_ — An outward shift of the entire curve caused by more or better resources, or technological progress.

#### Circular Flow of Income

- **Circular flow of income** — Shows the movement of income, output, and expenditure between the five sectors of the economy.

- **Sectors of the economy** — Households, Firms, Government, Banks/financial sector, Foreign sector.

- **Leakages** — Income leaving the circular flow:
  - _Savings (S)_
  - _Taxes (T)_
  - _Imports (M)_

- **Injections** — Spending entering the circular flow:
  - _Investment (I)_
  - _Government spending (G)_
  - _Exports (X)_

### 1.2 How Do Economists Approach the World?

#### Positive vs. Normative Economics

- **Positive economics** — Factual, objective, and testable statements about what _is_.

- **Normative economics** — Value judgments or opinion-based statements about what _should_ be.

|                  | Positive                           | Normative                              |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| **What it is**   | Factual, objective, testable       | Value judgment, opinion-based          |
| **Key question** | What _is_?                         | What _should_ be?                      |
| **Example**      | "Higher taxes reduce consumption." | "The government _should_ raise taxes." |

- **Other key approaches and distinctions:**
  - _Ceteris paribus_ — "All else equal." Used to isolate the effect of one variable in a model.
  - _Refutation_ — A valid hypothesis must be falsifiable by real-world evidence.

#### Economic Thought (Brief Overview)

- **18th century — Adam Smith** — Advocated _laissez-faire_; free markets self-regulate through the "invisible hand."

- **19th century — Classical economics** — Developed ideas such as marginal utility and Say's Law ("supply creates its own demand"), while also prompting Marxist critiques of capitalism.

- **20th century — Keynes and monetarists** — Keynes argued that markets can fail and that government fiscal stimulus may be needed in recessions. Later, monetarists such as Friedman challenged this emphasis and stressed inflation control and market self-correction.

- **21st century developments** — Behavioural economics integrates psychology with economics, while modern economics places increasing emphasis on sustainability, social interdependence, and the _circular economy_ (reuse/recycle rather than linear take-make-dispose).

## Unit 2: Microeconomics

### 2.1 Demand

- **Law of demand** — As price rises, quantity demanded falls, _ceteris paribus_. The demand curve slopes downward from left to right.
  - **Income effect** — When price rises, consumers feel poorer and buy less of the good.
  - **Substitution effect** — When price rises, consumers switch to now-relatively-cheaper substitutes.
  - **Law of diminishing marginal utility** — Each additional unit of a good consumed gives less extra satisfaction than the previous one, so consumers only buy more at a lower price.
  - **HL: Assumptions underlying the law of demand** — Rationality (consumers maximise utility), perfect information, and well-defined, stable preferences.

- **Individual vs. market demand** — Market demand is the horizontal summation of all individual consumers' demand curves at each price.

- **Non-price determinants of demand** — Shift the entire demand curve (≠ movement along):
  - _Income_ — ↑ income → ↑ demand for normal goods; ↓ demand for inferior goods.
  - _Tastes and preferences_ — Fashions, advertising, or social trends shift demand.
  - _Future price expectations_ — If consumers expect prices to rise, they buy more now.
  - _Price of related goods_ — Rise in price of substitute → ↑ demand for the good; rise in price of complement → ↓ demand for the good.
  - _Number of consumers_ — A larger market population increases market demand.

- **Movements along vs. shifts** — A price change causes a _movement along_ the curve (change in quantity demanded). A non-price determinant change causes a _shift_ of the curve (change in demand).

### 2.2 Supply

- **Law of supply** — As price rises, quantity supplied rises, _ceteris paribus_. The supply curve slopes upward.
  - **Law of diminishing marginal returns** — Adding successive units of a variable factor (e.g. labour) to a fixed factor (e.g. capital) eventually yields smaller additions to output, driving up marginal cost.
  - **Increasing marginal costs** — Because marginal costs rise as output increases, producers require higher prices to supply more, producing the upward slope.
  - **HL: Assumptions underlying the law of supply** — Rational profit-maximising firms, ceteris paribus conditions on all other factors.

- **Individual vs. market supply** — Market supply is the horizontal summation of all individual firms' supply curves.

- **Non-price determinants of supply** — Shift the supply curve:
  - _Costs of factors of production (FOPs)_ — ↑ input costs → supply decreases (curve shifts left).
  - _Prices of related goods_ — Joint supply: producing more beef → more leather. Competitive supply: higher wheat prices may reduce barley production.
  - _Indirect taxes_ — Treated as a cost; reduce supply. Subsidies reduce costs; increase supply.
  - _Future price expectations_ — Firms may hold back supply now if they expect higher prices later.
  - _Technology_ — Improvements reduce costs and increase supply.
  - _Number of firms_ — More firms in the market increases market supply.

- **Movements along vs. shifts** — Same logic as demand: price → movement along; non-price → shift.

### 2.3 Competitive Market Equilibrium

- **Market equilibrium** — The price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. No tendency for price to change. At any other price there is either a _shortage_ (excess demand: QD > QS, price rises) or a _surplus_ (excess supply: QS > QD, price falls).

\centering
\textbf{Fig 2.3a — Market Equilibrium}\par
\vspace{0.35em}
\includegraphics[width=0.64\textwidth]{graphs/2_3a_market_equilibrium.png}\par
\vspace{0.35em}
\emph{Shows demand and supply intersecting at equilibrium price and quantity.}
\end{figure}

- **Functions of the price mechanism** — Prices coordinate resource allocation in a market economy:
  - _Signalling_ — Rising prices signal to producers that more output is wanted; falling prices signal the opposite.
  - _Incentive_ — Higher prices incentivise producers to expand supply and consumers to reduce consumption.
  - _Rationing_ — Prices ration scarce goods to those willing and able to pay, clearing excess demand.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\textbf{Fig 2.3b — Change in Equilibrium After a Demand Shift}\par
\vspace{0.35em}

\vspace{0.35em}
\emph{Shows how a shift in demand changes \(P^*\) and \(Q^*\).}

- **Consumer surplus** — The difference between what consumers are _willing to pay_ and what they _actually pay_. It is the area above the price line and below the demand curve.

- **Producer surplus** — The difference between the price producers _receive_ and the minimum price they were _willing to accept_. Area below the price line and above the supply curve.

- **Social surplus** — CS + PS. Maximised at the competitive equilibrium, meaning resources are allocated efficiently. `Social surplus = CS + PS`

- **Allocative efficiency** — Achieved when Marginal Benefit (MB) = Marginal Cost (MC), i.e. when the value consumers place on the last unit equals its cost of production. This occurs at the competitive market equilibrium.

### 2.4 Critique of Maximizing Behaviour _(HL only)_

- **Rational consumer choice** — Standard economic theory assumes consumers are fully rational: they maximise utility with perfect information and consistent preferences. However, real-world behaviour often contradicts this.

- **Behavioural economics** — Studies how psychological factors cause systematic deviations from rational choice:
  - _Anchoring and framing_ — People's decisions are influenced by how a choice is presented (e.g. a discount framed as "save £10" vs "10% off" affects choices differently).
  - _Availability bias_ — People overweight recent or easily recalled information (e.g. over-insuring against rare but memorable disasters).
  - _Rule of thumb_ — Using simple mental shortcuts (heuristics) instead of full cost-benefit analysis.
  - _Bounded rationality_ — People have limited cognitive capacity; they "satisfice" (find a good-enough solution) rather than optimise.
  - _Bounded self-control_ — People act against their own long-term interests (e.g. over-eating, over-spending).
  - _Bounded selfishness_ — People exhibit fairness and altruism, deviating from pure self-interest.
  - _Imperfect information_ — Consumers lack full information about product quality, price, or consequences.

- **Behavioural economics in action**:
  - _Choice architecture_ — Designing the environment in which choices are made to influence outcomes. Types: _default choices_ (organ donation opt-out vs. opt-in), _restricted choices_ (limiting available options), _mandated choices_ (legally required decisions).
  - _Nudge theory (Thaler & Sunstein)_ — Altering the choice environment in a non-coercive way to "nudge" people toward better decisions (e.g. placing healthy food at eye level in school canteens).

- **Business objectives** — Firms do not always maximise profit; alternative objectives include:
  - _Corporate social responsibility (CSR)_ — Prioritising ethical conduct and stakeholder welfare alongside profit.
  - _Market share_ — Maximising the firm's proportion of total industry sales, even at the cost of short-run profit.
  - _Satisficing_ — Achieving a satisfactory (not maximum) level of profit to satisfy key stakeholders (Simon, 1957).
  - _Growth_ — Maximising revenue or assets, often driven by managerial self-interest (empire-building).

### 2.5 Elasticities of Demand

- **Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)** — Measures the _responsiveness of quantity demanded_ to a change in price.

  $$
  PED = (\% \Delta Q_d) / (\% \Delta P)
  $$
  - Values: |PED| > 1 = _elastic_ (responsive); |PED| < 1 = _inelastic_ (unresponsive); |PED| = 1 = _unit elastic_; |PED| = ∞ = perfectly elastic; |PED| = 0 = perfectly inelastic. PED is always negative (law of demand) but discussed in absolute terms.
  - **HL: Changing PED along a straight-line demand curve** — PED changes along a linear demand curve: elastic in the upper half, unit elastic at the midpoint, inelastic in the lower half. This is because % changes differ at each point even though the absolute slope is constant.
  - **Determinants of PED:**
    - Number and closeness of substitutes — More substitutes → more elastic.
    - Degree of necessity — Necessities are inelastic; luxuries are elastic.
    - Proportion of income spent — Larger share of income → more elastic.
    - Time — Demand tends to become more elastic over time as consumers find substitutes.
  - **PED and total revenue (TR = P × Q):**
    - Elastic demand: ↑P → ↓TR (% fall in Qd > % rise in P)
    - Inelastic demand: ↑P → ↑TR (% fall in Qd < % rise in P)
    - Unit elastic: ↑P → TR unchanged
  - **HL: Primary commodities vs. manufactured goods** — Primary commodities tend to have lower PED (few substitutes, necessities, homogeneous) while manufactured goods often have more substitutes and are less of a necessity.

- **Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)** — Measures _responsiveness of quantity demanded_ to a change in income.
  $$
  YED = (\% \Delta Q_d) / (\% \Delta \text{income})
  $$
  - Positive YED = _normal good_ (demand rises with income)
  - Negative YED = _inferior good_ (demand falls as income rises, e.g. own-brand goods)
  - 0 < YED < 1 = income inelastic (necessities, e.g. bread)
  - YED > 1 = income elastic (luxuries/services, e.g. foreign holidays)
  - **HL: Importance of YED** — Firms in income-elastic industries benefit from economic growth; as an economy develops, demand shifts from income-inelastic primary sectors to income-elastic service sectors (explaining structural change).

### 2.6 Elasticity of Supply

- **Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)** — Measures the _responsiveness of quantity supplied_ to a change in price.
  $$
  PES = (\% \Delta Q_s) / (\% \Delta P)
  $$
  - PES is always positive (law of supply). Values: >1 elastic, <1 inelastic, =1 unitary, =∞ perfectly elastic, =0 perfectly inelastic.
  - **Determinants of PES:**
    - _Time_ — Very short run: supply is perfectly inelastic. Short run: some flexibility. Long run: most elastic as firms can fully adjust capacity.
    - _Mobility of factors of production_ — Easily moveable factors allow faster supply response.
    - _Unused capacity_ — Firms with spare capacity can increase output quickly (more elastic).
    - _Ability to store_ — Goods that can be stockpiled have more elastic supply.
    - _Rate at which costs increase_ — If marginal costs rise sharply, supply is less elastic.
  - **HL: Primary commodities vs. manufactured goods** — Primary goods (e.g. agricultural produce) have inelastic supply due to long production cycles and land constraints. Manufactured goods are more elastic as factories can ramp up production.

### 2.7 Role of Government in Microeconomics

- **Reasons for government intervention** — Governments intervene to: earn revenue, support firms, support low-income households, influence production or consumption levels, correct market failures, and promote equity.

- **Price ceiling (maximum price)** — A legally imposed maximum price _below_ the equilibrium price. Intended to keep goods affordable (e.g. rent controls, price caps on food in crises). **Consequences:** Creates a _shortage_ (QD > QS), black markets may emerge, non-price rationing may be needed (queuing), lower quality may result.
  - Effective only if set _below_ equilibrium price.

- **Price floor (minimum price)** — A legally imposed minimum price _above_ the equilibrium price. ### 2.8 Market Failure — Externalities & Common Pool Resources

- **Allocative efficiency** — Achieved when MSB = MSC (social surplus maximised). Market failure arises when the market equilibrium does not occur at MSB = MSC.

- **Externality** — A cost or benefit that falls on a _third party_ not involved in the transaction, causing a divergence between private and social costs/benefits.
  - `MSC = MPC + MEC` (marginal external cost)
  - `MSB = MPB + MEB` (marginal external benefit)

- **Negative externality of production** — Production imposes costs on third parties (e.g. factory pollution harming nearby residents). Market overproduces (Q_market > Q_social optimum). MSC > MPC → welfare loss (deadweight loss triangle between Q_social and Q_market).

- **Merit goods** — Goods with positive externalities of consumption that the market underprovides (e.g. education, healthcare). Often also subject to information failure.

- **Demerit goods** — Goods with negative externalities of consumption that the market overprovides (e.g. tobacco, alcohol).

- **Common pool (common access) resources** — Resources that are _rivalrous_ (one person's use reduces availability) but _non-excludable_ (no one can be prevented from using them). Examples: fish stocks, groundwater, clean air.
  - _Tragedy of the commons (Hardin, 1968)_: Self-interested rational actors deplete shared resources because the cost of overuse is spread across everyone while the benefit is captured individually, leading to unsustainable exploitation.

- **Government responses to externalities:**
  - _Pigouvian (indirect) taxes_ — Tax equal to the marginal external cost, shifting MPC up to MSC. Internalises the externality. E.g. carbon taxes on emissions.
  - _Carbon taxes_ — Specific type of Pigouvian tax placing a price on CO₂ emissions to reduce their production to the social optimum.
  - _Tradable permits (cap and trade)_ — Government sets a total emissions cap, issues permits, and lets firms trade them. Creates a market price for pollution; efficient firms sell surplus permits to less efficient ones.
  - _Legislation and regulation_ — Prohibits or limits harmful activities (e.g. emission standards, bans on certain chemicals).
  - _Education and awareness_ — Addresses information failure component; changes behaviour over time (e.g. anti-smoking campaigns).
  - _International agreements_ — For global externalities (e.g. climate change) where unilateral action is insufficient. E.g. Paris Agreement.
  - _Collective self-governance (Ostrom, 1990)_ — Communities can manage common resources effectively through shared rules and enforcement without government intervention — challenges Hardin's pessimistic view.
  - _Subsidies / government provision_ — For positive externalities, subsidies shift MPC down to MSC, or government directly provides the good.

### 2.9 Market Failure — Public Goods

- **Public goods** — Goods characterised by two properties that prevent the market from providing them:
  - _Non-rivalrous_ — One person's consumption does not reduce availability to others (e.g. a lighthouse beam).
  - _Non-excludable_ — It is impossible (or too costly) to prevent people from consuming the good once it is provided.

- **Free rider problem** — Because public goods are non-excludable, individuals have an incentive to consume without paying, making it unprofitable for private firms to provide them. The market will underprovide or not provide at all. → Justifies government provision.

- **Government responses:**
  - _Direct provision_ — Government funds and provides public goods from taxation (e.g. national defence, street lighting, flood defences).
  - _Contracting out_ — Government funds the good but hires a private firm to produce it; introduces market incentives while ensuring provision.

**Note:** A "quasi-public good" has some but not all characteristics of a pure public good (e.g. roads can become congested = rivalrous).

### 2.10 Market Failure — Asymmetric Information _(HL only)_

- **Asymmetric information** — When one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other, leading to market inefficiency.

- **Adverse selection** — Occurs _before_ a transaction when the less-informed party cannot distinguish between high- and low-quality options. Example: in insurance markets, people with higher risk are more likely to buy insurance; insurers cannot tell them apart → only high-risk customers buy, raising premiums and potentially causing market failure ("lemons problem," Akerlof 1970).

- **Moral hazard** — Occurs _after_ a transaction when one party takes on more risk because they know the other party bears the consequences. Example: someone with full car insurance may drive more recklessly because they won't bear the full cost of an accident.

- **Government responses:**
  - _Legislation and regulation_ — Mandate disclosure of information (e.g. food labelling laws, financial product prospectuses).
  - _Provision of information_ — Government campaigns to inform consumers (e.g. health warnings, energy efficiency ratings).

- **Private responses:**
  - _Signalling_ — The better-informed party credibly reveals information to reduce asymmetry (e.g. job applicants providing qualifications; firms offering warranties on products).
  - _Screening_ — The less-informed party finds ways to obtain information (e.g. insurance companies asking medical questions; employers using probationary periods).

### 2.11 Market Failure — Market Power _(HL only)_

- **Market structures overview:**
  - _Perfect competition_: Many firms, homogeneous products, free entry/exit, perfect information → no market power.
  - _Monopoly_: Single or dominant firm, high barriers to entry, no close substitutes → maximum market power.
  - _Oligopoly_: Few large firms, high barriers to entry, interdependence → significant market power.
  - _Monopolistic competition_: Many firms, free entry, differentiated products → limited market power.

- **Profit maximisation rule (HL):** Firms maximise profit by producing where `MC = MR`.
  - _Total approach_: `Profit = TR − TC`. Maximised at the output level where the gap is greatest.
  - _Marginal approach_: Produce as long as MR > MC; stop when MR = MC.
  - _Abnormal (supernormal) profit_: `AR > AC` (P > ATC) → profit per unit exists; TR > TC.
  - _Normal profit_: `AR = AC` (P = ATC) → TR = TC; just enough to keep firms in the industry.
  - _Losses_: `AR < AC` (P < ATC) → TR < TC. Firm may shut down if P < AVC in the short run.

- **Perfect competition:**
  - Firm is a _price taker_: `P = D = AR = MR`. Horizontal demand curve at the market price.
  - Short run: Can earn abnormal profit, normal profit, or losses.
  - Long run: Entry of new firms (if abnormal profit) or exit (if losses) drives the industry to _normal profit_. `P = MC = min ATC` → allocative and productive efficiency.

- **Monopoly:**
  - Firm faces the entire (downward-sloping) market demand; `MR < AR` at all output levels.
  - Produces at MC = MR, but charges the price on the demand curve (AR) → `P > MC` → allocative inefficiency.
  - Creates a _welfare loss_ (deadweight loss) compared to perfect competition: lower output, higher price.
  - _Natural monopoly_: Occurs when long-run average costs (LRAC) fall continuously over the relevant range of demand. One large firm can supply the entire market at lower cost than multiple smaller firms (e.g. utilities). Average cost pricing or government ownership may be used to regulate.

- **Oligopoly:**
  - Firms are _interdependent_: each firm's decisions affect rivals, who react in turn.
  - _Collusive oligopoly_: Firms agree (explicitly or tacitly) to set prices/output like a monopoly. Illegal in most jurisdictions (cartel).
  - _Non-collusive_: Firms compete; risk of _price wars_. The _kinked demand curve_ model shows price rigidity: each firm fears rivals will match price cuts but not price rises.
  - _Game theory (prisoners' dilemma)_: Even if collusion is profitable for all, each firm has an incentive to _cheat_ (lower price to gain market share), leading to a Nash equilibrium with lower profits — justifying the instability of cartels.
  - _Concentration ratio_: Measures the combined market share of the top N firms (e.g. 4-firm CR = % share of top 4). High CR indicates oligopolistic structure.

  - Long run: Free entry erodes profit → normal profit. Firm produces at `P > MC` → allocative inefficiency. Excess capacity exists.
  - Less market power than monopoly due to _many substitutes_ → more elastic demand curve.

  - _Legislation/regulation_ — Competition law, anti-trust regulation (e.g. EU competition policy).

### 2.12 The Market's Inability to Achieve Equity _(HL only)_

- **Equity vs. efficiency** — Free markets may achieve allocative efficiency but not necessarily equity. The distribution of income and wealth depends on ownership of factors of production, which is itself unequally distributed. High returns to capital and skilled labour naturally diverge from returns to unskilled labour.

- **Circular flow and inequality** — In a free market, factor rewards (wages, rent, interest, profit) flow to those who own the factors of production. Those with more/better capital, land, or human capital receive higher incomes, perpetuating inequality across generations.

## Unit 3: Macroeconomics

### 3.1 Measuring Economic Activity and Illustrating Its Variations

- **National income accounting** — A system for measuring the total economic activity of a country over a given period (usually one year). Three equivalent approaches:
  - _Expenditure approach_: `GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)`
  - _Income approach_: Sum of all factor incomes (wages + rent + interest + profit)
  - _Output approach_: Sum of value added by all producers
  - All three give the same result — the _circular flow of income_ ensures equivalence.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\textbf{Fig 3.1b — Circular Flow with Leakages and Injections}\par
\vspace{0.35em}
\includegraphics[width=0.64\textwidth]{graphs/3_1b_circular_flow_leakages.png}\par
\vspace{0.35em}
\emph{Shows households, firms, and the leakages/injections in the economy.}
\end{figure}

- **Gross Domestic Product (GDP)** — The total monetary value of all _final_ goods and services produced _within a country's borders_ in a given period. Does not include output by nationals abroad.

  $$
  GDP \text{ (expenditure)} = C + I + G + (X - M)
  $$

- **Gross National Income (GNI)** — The total income _received by a country's residents_, wherever they are located. Adjusts GDP for income flows crossing borders.

  $$
  GNI = GDP + \text{net income from abroad}
  $$

  Factor income received from abroad minus factor income sent abroad.

- **Nominal vs. Real GDP/GNI** — Nominal values use _current prices_ and can be misleading if prices change. Real values adjust for inflation using a _price deflator_:

  $$
  \text{Real GDP} = (\text{Nominal GDP} / \text{Price Deflator}) \times 100
  $$
  - This allows meaningful comparisons over time.

- **GDP/GNI per capita** — Divides total output by population to give a measure of average income per person. Better for comparing living standards across countries.

  $$
  \text{GDP per capita} = GDP / \text{Population}
  $$

- **Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)** — Adjusts income figures to reflect differences in price levels across countries. PPP-adjusted figures show what income can actually _buy_ in each country, enabling more meaningful international comparisons.

- **Business cycle** — The short-term fluctuations in real GDP around the long-term potential output (trend growth rate). Phases: _expansion (boom)_ → _peak_ → _contraction (recession)_ → _trough_ → recovery. A recession is commonly defined as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\textbf{Fig 3.1a — Business Cycle}\par
\vspace{0.35em}
\includegraphics[width=0.64\textwidth]{graphs/3_1a_business_cycle.png}\par
\vspace{0.35em}
\emph{Shows boom, recession, trough, recovery, and trend growth.}
\end{figure}

- **Limitations of GDP/GNI as a welfare measure** — GDP/GNI fail to capture: income distribution, unpaid work (e.g. household labour), environmental degradation, informal economy activity, quality of life, leisure time.

- **Alternative measures of well-being:**
  - _OECD Better Life Index_ — 11 dimensions including income, jobs, education, health, environment, civic engagement.
  - _Happiness Index (World Happiness Report)_ — Based on survey data on subjective well-being, incorporating social support, freedom, and generosity.
  - _Happy Planet Index_ — Measures sustainable well-being: `HPI = (wellbeing × life expectancy × inequality) / ecological footprint`. Rewards high well-being achieved with low environmental cost.

### 3.2 Variations in Economic Activity — AD and AS

- **Aggregate Demand (AD)** — The total planned spending on an economy's output at different price levels. Slopes _downward_ because: higher price level → real wealth falls (wealth effect), interest rates rise (interest rate effect), and exports become less competitive (exchange rate effect).

  $$
  AD = C + I + G + (X - M)
  $$

- **Determinants of AD components:**
  - _C (Consumption)_: consumer confidence, interest rates (↑ rates → ↓ C), wealth, income taxes, household debt, expected future prices.
  - _I (Investment)_: interest rates (↑ rates → ↓ I, as borrowing is costlier), business confidence, technology, business taxes, corporate debt.
  - _G (Government spending)_: political and fiscal priorities.
  - _X − M (Net exports)_: trading partners' income, exchange rates, trade policies.

- **Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)** — The total planned output by all producers at different price levels _in the short run_ (some input prices, especially wages, are fixed). Slopes _upward_: higher price level → higher producer revenues → more output supplied, as input costs haven't fully adjusted.
  - Determinants (shifts of SRAS): costs of FOPs (especially wages), indirect taxes, supply shocks (e.g. oil price rises).

- **Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS) — Monetarist/New Classical view** — A _vertical_ line at the _full employment level of output_ (potential output, Y_f). In the long run, prices and wages are fully flexible, so the economy always returns to Y_f. The LRAS can only shift through changes in productive capacity (more/better factors of production, technology, institutions).

- **Keynesian AS curve** — _Horizontal_ in the short run (or at low output levels) when there is substantial spare capacity (unemployed resources), then upward-sloping as the economy approaches capacity, and vertical at full capacity. Key implication: the economy can remain stuck below full employment, as wages are downwardly inflexible — it does _not_ automatically self-correct.

\centering
\textbf{Fig 3.2a — AD and AS Basics}\par
\vspace{0.35em}
\includegraphics[width=0.64\textwidth]{graphs/3_2a_AD_AS.png}\par
\vspace{0.35em}
\emph{Shows AD, SRAS, and macroeconomic equilibrium.}
\end{figure}

- **Macroeconomic equilibrium:**
  - _Short-run_: AD intersects SRAS. Can occur below, at, or above full employment.
  - _Long-run (Monetarist/New Classical)_: Automatic adjustment. If AD is below Y_f → wages fall → SRAS shifts right → economy returns to Y_f (natural rate of unemployment). Government intervention not needed.
  - _Keynesian long-run_: The economy can be stuck in a _deflationary/recessionary gap_ indefinitely due to sticky wages. Active government intervention (fiscal policy) is needed.
  - _Inflationary gap_: AD is above Y_f → upward pressure on prices.
  - _Deflationary/recessionary gap_: AD is below Y_f → unemployment above the natural rate.

### 3.3 Macroeconomic Objectives

#### Economic Growth

- **Definition** — A sustained increase in a country's real GDP over time; measured as the annual % change in real GDP.
  $$
  \text{Growth rate} = ((GDP_t - GDP_{t-1}) / GDP_{t-1}) \times 100
  $$
  - _Short-term (actual) growth_: Using existing spare capacity; shown as movement from inside to the frontier on a PPC, or rightward shift of AD in AD/AS.
  - _Long-term (potential) growth_: Expanding the productive capacity; shown as outward shift of the PPC or rightward shift of LRAS.
- **Consequences of growth:** ↑ living standards, ↑ government tax revenues, potential for investment; but also: environmental degradation (↑ resource use, emissions), potential ↑ income inequality (if growth is uneven).

#### Low Unemployment

- **Unemployment rate** — The percentage of the labour force that is actively seeking work but cannot find it.
  $$
  \text{Unemployment rate} = (\text{Unemployed} / \text{Labour force}) \times 100
  $$
  - _Labour force_ = employed + unemployed (excludes: retired, students, those not seeking work).
- **Types of unemployment:**
  - _Cyclical (demand-deficient)_: Caused by a fall in AD during a recession.
  - _Structural_: Caused by mismatch between workers' skills and available jobs (e.g. from deindustrialisation or technological change).
  - _Seasonal_: Caused by regular, predictable fluctuations in demand (e.g. tourism, agriculture).
  - _Frictional_: Caused by the time lag between leaving one job and finding another; often short-term.
- **Natural rate of unemployment (NRU)** = Structural + Frictional unemployment. The unemployment that exists even at "full employment." Represents a labour market in equilibrium.
- **Costs of unemployment:** Personal (loss of income, mental health, skill atrophy); social (crime, social exclusion); economic (lost output, higher government spending on benefits, lower tax revenue).

#### Low and Stable Inflation

- **Consumer Price Index (CPI)** — Measures the average change in prices of a _basket of goods and services_ representative of typical household spending.
  $$
  CPI = (\text{Cost of basket in current year} / \text{Cost of basket in base year}) \times 100
  $$
  $$
  \text{Inflation rate} = ((CPI_t - CPI_{t-1}) / CPI_{t-1}) \times 100
  $$
- **Limitations of CPI:** Does not account for substitution behaviour; basket may not represent all households; ignores quality improvements; excludes asset prices (housing).
- **Demand-pull inflation** — Caused by excessive AD relative to AS (the economy is "overheating"). Too much money chasing too few goods. `AD ↑ → P ↑`
- **Cost-push inflation** — Caused by a rise in production costs (e.g. oil price shocks, wage demands) shifting SRAS _left_. `SRAS ↓ → P ↑, output ↓` (stagflation risk).
- **Costs of high inflation:** Uncertainty (discourages investment), redistributive effects (debtors gain, fixed-income earners lose), erosion of savings, reduced export competitiveness, resource misallocation (menu costs, shoe-leather costs).
- **Deflation** — A sustained fall in the general price level. Causes: fall in AD (demand-side deflation, e.g. recession) or rise in AS (supply-side deflation, e.g. technology).
- **Costs of deflation:** Consumers defer spending (expecting further price falls) → ↓ AD → recession; ↑ real value of debt → more defaults; association with high unemployment; policy becomes less effective (interest rates cannot go below zero — the _zero lower bound_).
- **Disinflation** — A _fall in the rate of inflation_ (prices still rising but more slowly). Not the same as deflation.

#### Sustainable Government Debt _(HL only)_

- **Government (national) debt** — The accumulated stock of past borrowing by the government that has not yet been repaid. Measured as a % of GDP for international comparisons.
  $$
  \text{Debt/GDP ratio} = (\text{Total govt debt} / \text{Nominal GDP}) \times 100
  $$
- **Budget deficit** — When government spending exceeds tax revenue in a given year. Each year's deficit adds to the total stock of national debt.
  $$
  \text{Budget deficit} = G - T
  $$
  If positive, there is a deficit; if negative, a surplus.
- **Costs of high national debt:** High _debt servicing costs_ (interest payments crowd out productive spending); risk of credit rating downgrades (higher future borrowing costs); future generations face higher taxes or lower public services; potential sovereign debt crisis.

#### Conflicts Between Macroeconomic Objectives

- **Low unemployment vs. low inflation (Phillips Curve):**
  - A.W. Phillips (1958) found an empirical inverse relationship between unemployment and wage inflation in the UK: when unemployment is low, wages (and prices) rise faster.
  - _Short-run Phillips curve (SRPC)_: Shows the trade-off; policymakers can "buy" lower unemployment at the cost of higher inflation (via expansionary policy) and vice versa.
  - **HL: Long-run Phillips curve (LRPC):** Vertical at the _natural rate of unemployment_. In the long run, there is no trade-off — attempts to keep unemployment below the NRU only lead to _accelerating inflation_ (Friedman's expectations-augmented model). The economy returns to the NRU in the long run regardless of the inflation rate.

### 3.4 Economics of Inequality and Poverty

- **Equality vs. equity** — _Equality_ means everyone has the same amount; _equity_ means fairness in distribution. These are not the same: an equitable distribution may tolerate inequality if it reflects effort or merit, but extreme inequality is generally considered inequitable.

- **Lorenz Curve** — A graphical representation of the _cumulative distribution of income_. The x-axis shows the cumulative % of population (ranked from poorest to richest); the y-axis shows the cumulative % of income received. The _line of perfect equality_ is a 45° diagonal. The further the Lorenz curve bows below this line, the greater the inequality.

- **Gini Coefficient** — A summary statistic derived from the Lorenz curve:

  $$
  Gini = \text{Area A} / (\text{Area A} + \text{Area B})
  $$
  - Where A = area between the line of perfect equality and the Lorenz curve; B = area below the Lorenz curve.
  - Range: 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (or 100%) = perfect inequality. Higher Gini = more inequality.
  - **HL: Construction** — Plot income quintile data cumulatively (e.g. poorest 20%, poorest 40%...) to draw the Lorenz curve.

- **Absolute poverty** — Living below a fixed standard of income needed for basic survival (e.g. World Bank international poverty line of $2.15 per day at PPP). Does not change as average income rises.

- **Relative poverty** — Living below a threshold defined relative to the median income of a country (e.g. 60% of median income). Can persist even as a country grows richer if inequality remains high.

- **Measuring poverty:**
  - _Single indicators_: International poverty lines, minimum income standards.
  - _Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)_: Captures deprivation across health, education, and living standards dimensions — not just income. Better reflects the multi-dimensional nature of poverty.

- **Causes of economic inequality and poverty:**
  - Inequality of opportunity (unequal access to education, healthcare)
  - Unequal ownership of capital and land (returns to capital concentrate wealth)
  - Unequal human capital (skills, education, experience)
  - Discrimination (gender, racial, social)
  - Unequal political power
  - Government tax and benefit structures
  - Globalisation and technological change (rewards high-skilled labour, displaces low-skilled)
  - Market-based supply-side policies (reduce redistribution mechanisms)

- **Impact of inequality on:** Economic growth (↑ inequality may reduce growth via ↓ social mobility and human capital investment); living standards (bottom quintiles may face poor health/education outcomes); social stability (↑ crime, political polarisation, social unrest).

- **Progressive, regressive, proportional taxes:**
  - _Progressive_: Average tax rate _rises_ with income (e.g. income tax with tax brackets). Reduces inequality.
  - _Regressive_: Average tax rate _falls_ as income rises (e.g. VAT — takes a larger % of poor people's income). Worsens inequality.
  - _Proportional_: Average tax rate stays constant regardless of income (e.g. flat tax).
  - `Average tax rate = Total tax paid / Total income × 100`
  - `Marginal tax rate = Tax on additional income / Additional income × 100`

\centering

### 3.5 Demand Management — Monetary Policy

- **Monetary policy** — Actions by the _central bank_ to influence the money supply, interest rates, and credit conditions to achieve macroeconomic objectives. The central bank is typically independent of government (e.g. Bank of England, ECB, Federal Reserve).

- **Goals:** Low and stable inflation (most central banks have an _inflation target_, e.g. 2% in the UK), low unemployment, smooth business cycle fluctuations, long-term growth, external balance.

- **HL: Money creation by commercial banks** — Commercial banks hold only a fraction of deposits as reserves (fractional reserve banking) and lend out the rest, creating new money. Each loan becomes a deposit elsewhere, which is again lent out — the _money multiplier_:

  $$
  \text{Money multiplier} = 1 / \text{Reserve ratio}
  $$

- **HL: Tools of monetary policy:**
  - _Open market operations (OMOs)_: Central bank buys (↑ money supply) or sells (↓ money supply) government bonds. Buying bonds injects money into the banking system and lowers interest rates.
  - _Minimum reserve requirements_: The mandatory % of deposits banks must hold. ↑ reserve ratio → ↓ lending capacity → ↓ money supply.
  - _Central bank minimum lending rate (base/discount/refinancing rate)_: The rate at which the central bank lends to commercial banks. ↓ base rate → ↓ commercial bank rates → ↑ borrowing and spending.
  - _Quantitative easing (QE)_: The central bank creates new money electronically and uses it to purchase long-term financial assets (e.g. government and corporate bonds), increasing the money supply and lowering long-term interest rates when conventional monetary policy is exhausted (interest rates near zero).

- **HL: Demand and supply of money — equilibrium interest rate:**
  - _Money demand_ slopes downward: at lower interest rates, people hold more money (lower opportunity cost).
  - _Money supply_ is vertical (controlled by the central bank).
  - Intersection gives equilibrium interest rate. Central bank can shift money supply to change the interest rate.

\centering
\textbf{Fig 3.5a — Money Market (HL)}\par

\includegraphics[width=0.64\textwidth]{graphs/3_5a_money_market.png}\par
\vspace{0.35em}
\emph{Shows vertical money supply, downward-sloping money demand, and the equilibrium interest rate.}
\end{figure}

- **Real vs. nominal interest rate:**

  $$
  \text{Real interest rate} \approx \text{Nominal interest rate} - \text{Inflation rate}
  $$

  Fisher equation.
  - Real rate matters for economic decisions (it reflects the true cost of borrowing).

- **Expansionary monetary policy** — ↓ interest rates → ↑ borrowing, ↑ consumption (C), ↑ investment (I) → AD shifts right → closes a deflationary/recessionary gap.
- **Contractionary monetary policy** — ↑ interest rates → ↓ borrowing, ↓ C, ↓ I → AD shifts left → closes an inflationary gap.

\end{figure}

- **Effectiveness of monetary policy:**
  - _Constraints_: Cannot push nominal rates below zero (zero lower bound); low confidence may mean businesses/consumers don't borrow even at low rates (liquidity trap); QE may increase asset prices without reaching real economy.
  - _Strengths_: Can be implemented quickly (incremental and flexible); easily reversible; relatively short time lags compared to fiscal policy; central bank independence reduces political interference.

### 3.6 Demand Management — Fiscal Policy

- **Fiscal policy** — Government decisions on taxation and spending to influence AD and achieve macroeconomic objectives.
  - _Sources of revenue_: Direct taxes (income tax, corporation tax), indirect taxes (VAT, excise), revenue from state-owned enterprises (SOEs), sale of government assets.
  - _Expenditures_: Current (day-to-day spending, e.g. public sector wages), capital (infrastructure, schools), transfer payments (benefits, pensions — not direct payments for output).

- **Expansionary fiscal policy** — ↑G and/or ↓T → ↑ AD → real output ↑ and unemployment ↓. Used to close a deflationary/recessionary gap.
- **Contractionary fiscal policy** — ↓G and/or ↑T → ↓ AD → price level ↓. Used to close an inflationary gap.

- **HL: Keynesian multiplier** — An initial injection into the circular flow creates a _larger_ total increase in national income, because each round of spending becomes income for someone else who then spends a fraction of it.
  $$
  \text{Multiplier} \ (k) = 1 / (1 - MPC)
  $$
  $$
  k = 1 / (MPS + MPT + MPM)
  $$
  - Where MPC = marginal propensity to consume, MPS = save, MPT = tax, MPM = import.
  - `ΔY = k × ΔG` (or ΔI or ΔX)
  - The multiplier is larger when MPC is high (people spend more of any extra income) and smaller when leakages (MPS, MPT, MPM) are large.

- **HL: Crowding out** — If government borrows heavily to finance expansionary fiscal policy, it competes with private sector borrowers for funds → ↑ interest rates → ↓ private investment (I). This partially offsets the stimulus effect of ↑G. Stronger when the economy is near full employment.

- **HL: Automatic stabilisers** — Features of fiscal policy that automatically dampen business cycle fluctuations without deliberate policy changes:
  - _Progressive income tax_: In a boom, tax revenue rises automatically (stabilising AD). In a recession, tax revenue falls automatically (cushioning the fall).
  - _Unemployment benefits_: Rise automatically in recessions (supporting household income and AD) and fall in booms.

- **Effectiveness of fiscal policy:**
  - _Constraints_: Long recognition, decision, and implementation time lags; political pressures (difficult to cut spending or raise taxes); risk of unsustainable debt; crowding out.
  - _Strengths_: Can target specific sectors/regions; government spending directly increases AD (no "transmission mechanism" needed); very effective in a deep recession when monetary policy is limited.

### 3.7 Supply-Side Policies

- **Supply-side policies** — Policies that aim to increase the _productive capacity_ of the economy (shift LRAS or Keynesian AS rightward), improving long-run growth, efficiency, and international competitiveness. They work on the _supply side_ (AS), not AD.

- **Market-based policies** — Reduce government intervention and allow market forces to operate more freely:
  - _Deregulation_: Removing rules that restrict competition (e.g. liberalising airline markets).
  - _Privatisation_: Transferring state-owned enterprises to the private sector to introduce market incentives and efficiency.
  - _Trade liberalisation_: Reducing tariffs and trade barriers to expose domestic firms to competition.
  - _Anti-monopoly regulation_: Breaking up monopolies or preventing anti-competitive mergers.
  - _Labour market flexibility_ — Policies to reduce structural unemployment and labour costs:
    - Reducing union bargaining power
    - Reducing unemployment benefits (increase incentive to work)
    - Abolishing minimum wages (reduce labour costs)
  - _Incentive-related policies_: Personal income tax cuts (increase incentive to work/earn more); business tax and capital gains tax cuts (incentivise investment).

- **Interventionist policies** — Use government spending to build productive capacity:
  - _Education and training_: Increase human capital → more productive labour force.
  - _Healthcare_: Healthier workers are more productive.
  - _Research and development (R&D)_: Subsidising innovation → technological progress → productivity growth.
  - _Infrastructure_: Transport, energy, telecommunications, water/sanitation — reduce business costs and improve connectivity.
  - _Industrial policies_: Targeted support for strategic sectors.

- **Demand-side effects of supply-side policies** — Some supply-side policies also increase AD: tax cuts raise household income (↑C) and corporate profits (↑I); infrastructure spending directly increases G.

- **Supply-side effects of fiscal policies** — Capital spending (e.g. on education, infrastructure) also shifts AS rightward in the long run, even though it appears in the short run as a fiscal policy measure.

- **Effectiveness:**
  - _Market-based constraints_: Equity concerns (wage cuts hurt low-income workers), long time lags, vested interests (unions, incumbent firms resist), potential environmental harm from deregulation.
  - _Interventionist constraints_: High fiscal cost, long time lags (e.g. education takes years to affect workforce quality).
  - _Market-based strengths_: Improved resource allocation, no direct burden on government budget (unlike interventionist).
  - _Interventionist strengths_: Directly targets sectors crucial for long-term growth; addresses market failures.

## Unit 4: The Global Economy

### 4.1 Benefits of International Trade

- **Benefits of international trade:**
  - _Increased competition_ — Domestic firms face competitive pressure → ↑ efficiency.
  - _Lower prices_ — Competition from imports reduces prices for consumers.
  - _Greater choice_ — Consumers access a wider variety of goods.
  - _Acquisition of resources_ — Countries can obtain resources they lack domestically.
  - _More foreign exchange earnings_ — Export revenue supports the balance of payments.
  - _Access to larger markets_ — Firms can sell beyond domestic market → ↑ revenues.
  - _Economies of scale_ — Larger markets allow firms to lower average costs via larger-scale production.
  - _More efficient resource allocation_ — Production concentrated in areas of comparative advantage.

- **Free trade with imports** — If the world price is below the domestic price, consumers gain from lower prices and greater choice while domestic producers lose market share.

- **Free trade with exports** — If the world price is above the domestic price, producers gain access to larger markets and export revenue, while domestic consumers face higher prices.

- **HL: Absolute advantage (Adam Smith)** — A country has an _absolute advantage_ in producing a good if it can produce more of it with the same resources, or the same amount with fewer resources, than another country. Trade should be based on each country producing what it makes most efficiently in absolute terms.

- **HL: Comparative advantage (David Ricardo)** — A country has a _comparative advantage_ in producing a good if it can produce it at a _lower opportunity cost_ than another country, even if it has an absolute disadvantage in both goods. Both countries benefit from specialisation and trade as long as their opportunity costs differ.
  - _Opportunity cost approach_: Compare the ratio of outputs (or inputs) in each country. The country with the lower opportunity cost of producing Good A should specialise in A.

    $$
    \text{OC of good A in Country 1} = \text{Units of B given up per unit of A produced}
    $$

  - Gains from trade: Both countries consume outside their individual PPCs by trading at a price ratio between their domestic opportunity costs.

- **HL: Limitations of comparative advantage theory:**
  - Assumes no transport costs, no trade barriers, and perfect factor mobility — unrealistic.
  - Assumes constant opportunity costs (linear PPC) — but most PPCs are concave.
  - Ignores economies of scale and dynamic comparative advantage (countries can develop new advantages over time).
  - Doesn't account for distribution of gains (winners and losers within countries).
  - Ignores terms of trade uncertainty.

### 4.3 Arguments For and Against Trade Protection

**Arguments FOR protection:**

- _Infant (sunrise) industry argument_ — New domestic industries may need temporary protection to develop economies of scale and become internationally competitive before facing full foreign competition. Justification for _temporary_ protection.
- _National security_ — Some industries (e.g. defence, food production) must be maintained domestically regardless of comparative advantage.
- _Health and safety / environmental standards_ — Keeping out goods produced below domestic standards.
- _Anti-dumping_ — Protecting against foreign firms selling below cost of production (predatory pricing) to undercut domestic rivals and later monopolise the market.
- _Unfair competition_ — Responding to foreign government subsidies that give overseas firms an artificial cost advantage.
- _Balance of payments correction_ — Restricting imports to reduce a current account deficit.
- _Government revenue_ — Tariffs provide revenue, especially relevant for developing economies with limited tax capacity.
- _Protection of jobs_ — Preventing import-led unemployment in sensitive industries (though this displaces inefficiency rather than eliminating it).
- _ELDC diversification_ — Economically least developed countries (ELDCs) may need to diversify away from primary commodities to reduce vulnerability to commodity price volatility.

**Arguments AGAINST protection:**

- _Misallocation of resources_ — Resources directed to protected (inefficient) industries away from areas of comparative advantage.
- _Retaliation_ — Trading partners may impose their own tariffs → escalating trade wars, benefiting no one.
- _Increased costs_ — Protected firms face less competitive pressure → remain inefficient; downstream industries pay higher input costs.
- _Higher prices for consumers_ — Tariffs and quotas raise domestic prices.
- _Less choice_ — Import restrictions reduce product variety.
- _Lack of incentive to become more efficient_ — Protected domestic firms may not innovate.
- _Reduced export competitiveness_ — If inputs become more expensive (due to protected upstream industries), export industries suffer.

### 4.4 Economic Integration

- **Preferential trade agreements** — Give member countries more favourable access than non-members:
  - _Bilateral_: Two countries grant each other preferential access.
  - _Regional_: A group of countries grant each other preferential access (e.g. ASEAN, NAFTA/USMCA, EU).

- **Multilateral trade agreements** — Rules negotiated through the WTO that apply equally to all member countries.

- **Trading blocs:**
  - _Free trade area (FTA)_: Members remove tariffs among themselves but maintain independent external tariffs (e.g. ASEAN Free Trade Area).
  - _Customs union_: FTA + common external tariff applied to non-members (e.g. Mercosur).
  - _Common market_: Customs union + free movement of labour and capital (e.g. EU Single Market).
  - _Economic union_: Common market + coordinated economic policies.
  - _Monetary union_: Common currency (e.g. Eurozone). No exchange rate between members.

- **HL: Trade creation vs. trade diversion:**
  - _Trade creation_: Trade bloc formation leads members to buy from lower-cost producers within the bloc instead of higher-cost domestic producers → efficiency gain. (+)
  - _Trade diversion_: The common external tariff redirects trade away from lower-cost non-member countries toward higher-cost member countries → efficiency loss. (−)

- **Monetary union:**
  - _Advantages_: Eliminates exchange rate uncertainty between members, reduces transaction costs, promotes price transparency, can attract investment.
  - **HL: Disadvantages:** Loss of independent monetary policy (a single monetary policy may be unsuitable for all member economies), loss of exchange rate adjustment mechanism, difficulty dealing with asymmetric shocks, may require fiscal transfers.

- **WTO — World Trade Organization:**
  - _Objectives_: Promote free trade, reduce barriers, provide a forum for trade negotiations, settle trade disputes.
  - _Functions_: Administers trade agreements, provides dispute resolution mechanism, monitors trade policies.
  - _Limitations_: Difficult to reach agreement (consensus needed among 164 members); unequal bargaining power favours developed nations; agricultural protectionism remains contentious; limited enforcement power.

### 4.5 Exchange Rates

- **Exchange rate** — The price of one currency expressed in terms of another (e.g. £1 = €1.15). Determined in the foreign exchange (forex) market by supply and demand for currencies.

- **Floating exchange rate** — The exchange rate is determined freely by market forces (demand and supply for the currency) with no government or central bank intervention.
  - _Appreciation_: The currency's value rises (demand for it increases or supply decreases). E.g. ↑ demand for UK exports → ↑ demand for £ → £ appreciates.
  - _Depreciation_: The currency's value falls.
  - **Factors shifting currency demand/supply:**
    - Foreign demand for exports (↑ exports → ↑ demand for domestic currency)
    - Domestic demand for imports (↑ imports → ↑ supply of domestic currency)
    - Inward FDI (↑ FDI → ↑ demand for domestic currency)
    - Portfolio investment flows (↑ inward investment → ↑ demand)
    - Remittances
    - Speculation (expectations of future exchange rate changes)
    - Relative inflation rates (higher inflation → currency depreciates due to ↓ competitiveness)
    - Relative interest rates (higher interest rates → capital inflows → currency appreciates)
    - Relative growth rates (faster growth → ↑ imports → ↓ exchange rate, but also ↑ investment inflows)
    - Central bank intervention

### 4.6 Balance of Payments

- **Balance of payments (BoP)** — A record of all financial transactions between a country and the rest of the world over a given period. By accounting convention, _total credits = total debits_ (the overall BoP must balance).

- **Current account** — Records flows of goods, services, income, and current transfers:
  - _Balance of trade in goods (visible trade)_: Exports − imports of physical goods.
  - _Balance of trade in services (invisible trade)_: Exports − imports of services (tourism, finance, insurance).
  - _Primary income_: Net flows of wages, dividends, and interest (factor income).
  - _Secondary income (current transfers)_: Net transfers (e.g. foreign aid, remittances).
  - _Current account balance = trade in goods + trade in services + primary income + secondary income_

- **Capital account** — Records capital transfers (e.g. debt forgiveness) and transactions in non-produced, non-financial assets (e.g. purchase of intellectual property rights).

- **Financial account** — Records investment flows:
  - _Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)_: Investment in physical assets (factories, companies) in another country.
  - _Portfolio investment_: Purchase of financial assets (shares, bonds) without controlling interest.
  - _Reserve assets_: Changes in the central bank's foreign exchange reserves.
  - _Official borrowing_: Government borrowing from abroad.

- **Interdependence of accounts:** A current account _deficit_ must be financed by a financial account _surplus_ (capital inflows). `Current account + Capital account + Financial account = 0` (with statistical adjustments).

- **HL: Persistent current account deficit — implications:**
  - _Exchange rate_: May cause currency depreciation (supply of domestic currency ↑).
  - _Interest rates_: May need to attract foreign capital via higher interest rates.
  - _Foreign ownership of domestic assets_: Financed by selling assets to foreigners (FDI, portfolio investment inflows).
  - _Debt_: Accumulation of external debt.
  - _Credit ratings_: May be downgraded, raising future borrowing costs.
  - _Demand management_: Contractionary policy to reduce AD and imports (but may hurt growth).
  - _Economic growth_: May be constrained by need to repay debt or cut spending.

- **HL: Methods to correct a persistent current account deficit:**
  - _Expenditure switching_ — Policies that shift domestic and foreign spending away from imports toward domestic goods (e.g. currency depreciation, tariffs).
  - _Expenditure reducing_ — Contractionary fiscal/monetary policy to reduce overall AD and therefore imports.
  - _Supply-side policies_ — Improve international competitiveness to boost exports and reduce import dependence over the long run.

- **HL: Marshall-Lerner condition and J-curve effect:**
  - _Marshall-Lerner condition_: For a currency _depreciation_ to improve the current account balance (reduce a deficit), the sum of the price elasticities of demand for exports and imports must be greater than 1:
    $$
    |PED_X| + |PED_M| > 1
    $$
  - If the condition is met, depreciation eventually improves the current account.
  - _J-curve effect_: In the short run after depreciation, the current account balance _worsens_ before it improves. This is because import and export quantities are slow to adjust (contracts locked in, consumers slow to respond) — so import prices rise immediately, worsening the trade balance. Over time, as quantities adjust, the balance improves — creating a J-shaped path when plotted over time.

- **HL: Persistent current account surplus — implications:**
  - _Domestic consumption/investment_: Resources channelled to exports rather than domestic use.
  - _Exchange rate_: Upward pressure on currency (↑ demand from export earnings).
  - _Inflation_: ↑ export demand may contribute to inflationary pressure.
  - _Employment_: Strong export sector supports employment, but trading partners may retaliate.
  - _Export competitiveness_: Currency appreciation may erode it over time.

### 4.7 Sustainable Development

- **Sustainable development** — Development that "meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs" (Brundtland Commission, 1987). Balances economic growth, social equity, and environmental protection.

- **Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)** — A framework of 17 global goals adopted by the UN in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda. Includes: no poverty, zero hunger, good health, quality education, clean water, decent work, reduced inequality, climate action, life below water, life on land.

- **HL: Relationship between sustainability and poverty:** Poverty can drive unsustainable resource use (e.g. deforestation for fuel or farmland) when people have no alternatives. At the same time, environmental degradation worsens poverty (e.g. loss of fisheries, cropland). Breaking this cycle requires addressing both simultaneously.

---

### 4.8 Measuring Development

- **Economic development** — A broader concept than economic growth. It encompasses improvements in income levels, health, education, freedom, equality, and sustainable use of resources — essentially improvement in overall human well-being.

- **Single indicators:**
  - _GDP/GNI per capita at PPP_ — Income measure adjusted for price levels; widely used but limited (ignores distribution, non-material well-being).
  - _Health indicators_: Life expectancy, infant mortality rate, access to clean water.
  - _Education indicators_: Literacy rate, mean years of schooling, school enrolment.
  - _Inequality indicators_: Gini coefficient, income share of poorest quintile.
  - _Energy indicators_: Energy use per capita, access to electricity.
  - _Environmental indicators_: CO₂ emissions per capita, deforestation rates.

- **Composite indicators:**
  - _Human Development Index (HDI)_: Developed by UNDP. Combines three dimensions:
    1. Health: Life expectancy at birth
    2. Education: Mean years of schooling + expected years of schooling
    3. Standard of living: GNI per capita at PPP

       $$
       \mathrm{HDI} = \sqrt[3]{\left(\text{Health index} \times \text{Education index} \times \text{Income index}\right)}
       $$

       Geometric mean.
    - Range: 0 to 1; higher = more developed.

  - _Gender Inequality Index (GII)_: Measures gender disparities across reproductive health, empowerment, and labour market participation.
  - _Inequality-Adjusted HDI (IHDI)_: Adjusts the HDI downward for inequality in each dimension. The "loss" in HDI due to inequality.
  - _Happy Planet Index (HPI)_: Measures sustainable well-being: `HPI = (Wellbeing × Life expectancy × Equality) / Ecological footprint`.

- **Possible relationship between growth and development** — Growth is _necessary but not sufficient_ for development. Growth can fund education, healthcare, and infrastructure (supporting development) but may not automatically reduce poverty or inequality. Development requires deliberate redistribution and investment in human capital.

---

### 4.9 Barriers to Economic Growth and/or Development

- **Poverty trap / poverty cycle** — Self-reinforcing cycle where poverty → low savings → low investment → low productivity → low income → poverty. Multiple linked factors can perpetuate each other (e.g. low income → poor nutrition → low educational attainment → low wages → low income).

- **Economic barriers:**
  - _Rising inequality_: Concentrates income/wealth, limits social mobility and human capital development of the poor.
  - _Lack of infrastructure/technology_: Poor roads, energy supply, and connectivity raise business costs and limit market access.
  - _Low human capital_: Inadequate healthcare → lower labour productivity; inadequate education → less skilled workforce.
  - _Dependence on primary sector_: Primary commodities have low PED and PES, volatile prices (Singer-Prebisch thesis: long-run terms of trade may deteriorate for commodity exporters), and limited scope for value-added growth.
  - _Lack of access to international markets_: Trade barriers by developed countries; inability to compete.
  - _Informal economy_: Large informal sectors mean low tax revenue for governments and poor labour protections.
  - _Capital flight_: Wealthy individuals and firms move capital abroad, reducing domestic investment.
  - _Indebtedness_: High debt servicing costs divert government revenues from productive spending.
  - _Geography_: Landlocked countries face higher transport costs; tropical climates increase disease burden and reduce agricultural productivity.

- **Political and social barriers:**
  - _Weak institutional framework_: Poor legal system (insecure property rights discourage investment), ineffective taxation (low revenue), inadequate banking system (no credit access).
  - _Gender inequality_: Exclusion of women from education and labour force reduces productivity and growth.
  - _Corruption / lack of good governance_: Diverts public resources, distorts incentives, deters foreign investment.
  - _Unequal political power_: Elites may block redistributive policies or reforms that threaten their interests.

### 4.10 Economic Growth and/or Development Strategies

- **Trade strategies:**
  - _Import substitution (ISI)_: Protecting domestic industries (tariffs, subsidies) to replace imported goods with domestically produced alternatives. Can develop industrial capacity but risks inefficiency.
  - _Export-led growth (export promotion)_: Focusing production on goods for export to earn foreign exchange and benefit from economies of scale. Successful in East Asia (e.g. the newly industrialising economies).
  - _Economic integration_: Joining regional blocs to access larger markets and attract FDI.

- **Diversification** — Reducing dependence on primary commodities by developing manufacturing and services sectors to reduce exposure to commodity price volatility.

- **Social enterprise** — Businesses that trade primarily for a social/environmental purpose, reinvesting profits to achieve development goals.

- **Market-based policies:**
  - _Trade liberalisation_: Opening borders to trade to benefit from comparative advantage.
  - _Privatisation_: Transfer SOEs to private sector for efficiency.
  - _Deregulation_: Remove bureaucratic barriers to business.

- **Interventionist policies:**
  - _Redistribution_: Progressive taxation, transfer payments, minimum wages.
  - _Merit goods provision_: Government-funded education, healthcare, infrastructure (energy, transport, telecoms, water and sanitation) — investments in human capital and social infrastructure.

- **Inward FDI** — Foreign companies building factories/businesses domestically. Brings capital, technology, management skills, and export market access. Risk: profits repatriated, and environmental/labour standards may be weakened.

- **Foreign aid:**
  - _Humanitarian aid_: Emergency relief for crises.
  - _Development aid_: Long-term investment in infrastructure, education, health.
  - _Debt relief_: Cancellation or restructuring of developing country debt (e.g. HIPC Initiative) to free up resources for development spending.
  - _Official Development Assistance (ODA)_: Concessional (low-interest or grant-based) aid from government donors. UN target: 0.7% of GNI.
  - _NGOs_: Non-governmental organisations delivering aid/development programmes (e.g. Oxfam, MSF).

- **Multilateral development assistance:**
  - _World Bank_: Provides loans and grants for development projects; focuses on poverty reduction.
  - _International Monetary Fund (IMF)_: Provides emergency financial support for countries facing balance of payments crises; often attached to _conditionality_ (structural adjustment requirements). Controversial: IMF conditions have sometimes worsened inequality.

- **Institutional change:**
  - _Microfinance_: Small loans to entrepreneurs without access to formal banking. Helps break poverty traps (Grameen Bank model). Critics: evidence on long-term impact mixed.
  - _Mobile banking_: Technology enables financial services in remote areas without brick-and-mortar banks (e.g. M-Pesa in Kenya).
  - _Women's empowerment_: Strong evidence that improving women's education, property rights, and political participation boosts development.
  - _Reducing corruption_: Strengthens institutions, improves public resource use, attracts FDI.
  - _Property rights / land rights_: Secure land ownership gives incentive to invest and improves access to credit (using land as collateral — De Soto's argument).
