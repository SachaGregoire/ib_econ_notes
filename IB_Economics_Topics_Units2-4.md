### 2.5 Elasticities of Demand

- Concept of elasticity
- Price elasticity of demand (PED)
  - Formula: PED = % Δ Qd / % Δ P
  - Degrees of PED — theoretical range of values
  - **Changing PED along a straight-line downward-sloping demand curve (HL only)**
  - Determinants: number/closeness of substitutes, necessity, proportion of income, time
  - Relationship between PED and total revenue
  - Importance of PED for firms and government decision-making
  - **Reasons why PED for primary commodities is generally lower than for manufactured products (HL only)**
- Income elasticity of demand (YED)
  - Formula: YED = % Δ Qd / % Δ income
  - Income elastic (services/luxury goods) vs. income inelastic (necessities)
  - Sign: positive YED (normal goods), negative YED (inferior goods)
  - Values: <1 (necessities), >1 (services/luxuries)
  - **Importance of YED: for firms; explaining changes in sectoral structure of economy (HL only)**
- Cross price elasticity of demand (XED) *(implied by substitutes/complements)*

📊 **Diagrams:**
- Relatively elastic and inelastic demand curves
- Constant PED — perfectly elastic, perfectly inelastic, unitary PED
- **PED along the straight-line demand curve (HL only)**
- Showing changes in revenue as price changes (elastic vs. inelastic)
- Engel curve showing income elastic, inelastic, and inferior goods

🧮 **Calculations:**
- PED, change in price, Qd, or total revenue from data
- YED, change in income or Qd from data

### 2.11 Market Failure — Market Power *(HL only)*

- Market structures
  - Perfect competition: many firms, free entry, homogeneous products
  - Monopoly: single/dominant firm, high barriers to entry, no close substitutes
  - Oligopoly: few large firms, high barriers to entry, interdependence
  - Monopolistic competition: many firms, free entry, product differentiation
- **Rational producer behaviour — profit maximization (HL only)**
  - TR − TC method
  - MC = MR method
  - Abnormal profit (AR > AC), normal profit (AR = AC), losses (AR < AC)
- Degrees of market power
  - Perfect competition — no market power, firm as price taker
    - Profit maximization: short run and long run
    - Allocative efficiency (P = MC, MB = MC)
  - Imperfect competition — varying degrees of market power, firm as price maker
- Monopoly
  - Profit maximization
  - Allocative inefficiency (market failure)
  - Welfare loss vs. perfect competition
  - Natural monopoly
- Oligopoly
  - Collusive vs. non-collusive
  - Interdependence, risk of price war, incentive to collude, incentive to cheat
  - Allocative inefficiency
  - Simple game theory payoff matrix
  - Price and non-price competition
  - Concentration ratios
- Monopolistic competition
  - Profit maximization: short run and long run
  - More elastic demand vs. monopoly
  - Allocative inefficiency but more product variety
- Advantages of large firms: economies of scale, R&D/innovation financing
- Risks of concentrated markets: output, price, consumer choice
- Government intervention: legislation/regulation, government ownership, fines

📊 **Diagrams:**
- Perfectly competitive firm as price taker (P = D = AR = MR)
- Perfectly competitive firm: abnormal profit, normal profit, losses
- Perfectly competitive market equilibrium (P = MC, maximum social/community surplus)
- Market power: AR > MC
- Monopolist: abnormal profit, normal profit, losses
- Monopoly vs. perfect competition (price/quantity comparison + welfare loss)
- Natural monopoly
- Collusive oligopoly acting as a monopoly
- Game theory payoff matrix
- Monopolistically competitive firm: abnormal profit, normal profit, losses
- Monopolistic competition demand curve (more elastic than monopoly)

### 3.7 Supply-Side Policies

  - Incentive-related policies: personal income tax cuts, cuts in business/capital gains tax
- Interventionist policies
  - Education and training
  - Health care quality, quantity, and access
  - Research and development
  - Infrastructure provision
  - Industrial policies
- Demand-side effects of supply-side policies
- Supply-side effects of fiscal policies
- Effectiveness
  - Market-based constraints: equity issues, time lags, vested interests, environmental impact
  - Interventionist constraints: costs, time lags
  - Market-based strengths: improved resource allocation, no burden on budget
  - Interventionist strengths: direct support for key growth sectors
